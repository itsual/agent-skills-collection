# Systematic Review

## Overview

A systematic review answers a defined question by finding, appraising, and synthesizing all relevant evidence using a predetermined protocol — reducing cherry-picking.

## When to Use

- High-stakes evidence summaries
- Academic and clinical evidence bases
- Policy and standards decisions
- Technical state-of-the-art with auditability needs

## Core Practices

- Register or document the protocol (question, criteria, search plan)
- Search multiple sources with recorded strategies
- Screen studies with dual review when possible
- Appraise quality/risk of bias
- Synthesize narratively or quantitatively (meta-analysis) as appropriate
- Report PRISMA-style flow and limitations

## Principles

- Protocol precedes results to limit bias
- Absence of evidence ≠ evidence of absence
- Quality of included studies bounds quality of conclusions
- Updates may be needed as literature moves

## Verification

- [ ] Protocol and criteria are explicit
- [ ] Search and screening are reproducible
- [ ] Conclusions respect study quality and heterogeneity
