# Whitepaper

## Overview

Whitepapers combine education with persuasion. They should be substantial enough to be useful while still guiding the reader toward a clear perspective.

## When to Use

- Thought leadership content
- Technical or solution marketing
- Explaining complex problems and approaches in depth
- Supporting high-consideration sales cycles

## Typical Structure

1. Title and abstract / executive summary
2. Introduction to the problem landscape
3. Current approaches and their limitations
4. Proposed approach or framework
5. Evidence, examples, or architecture
6. Benefits and considerations
7. Conclusion and call to action
8. References

## Principles

- Lead with insight, not product features
- Support claims with evidence or clear reasoning
- Write for a knowledgeable but busy reader
- Keep a consistent, credible tone
- Make the document usable as a standalone resource

## Verification

- [ ] Reader gains genuine understanding of the topic
- [ ] Point of view is clear without being purely salesy
- [ ] Structure supports both skimming and deep reading
